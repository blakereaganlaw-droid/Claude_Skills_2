## Income statement structure (accrual)
```
Revenue
− Cost of goods sold
= Gross profit
− Operating expenses (SG&A, R&D, depreciation, etc.)
= Operating income (EBIT)
± Non-operating items (interest expense/income, other)
= Pre-tax income
− Income tax expense
= Net income
```
Covers a period. Recognizes revenue when earned and expense when incurred, independent of cash movement.

## Balance sheet structure (as of a date)
```
Assets                          Liabilities
  Current                         Current
    Cash                            Accounts payable
    Accounts receivable             Accrued liabilities
    Inventory                       Current portion of debt
    Prepaid expenses              Non-current
  Non-current                       Long-term debt
    PP&E (net of accum. dep.)     Equity
    Intangibles                     Contributed capital / common stock
    Long-term investments           Retained earnings
```
Identity: **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**. Retained earnings = beginning RE + net income − dividends.

## Cash flow statement — indirect method (template)
```
Operating activities
  Net income
  + Depreciation & amortization           (non-cash)
  + Stock-based compensation              (non-cash)
  ± Gains/losses on asset sales           (reverse; the cash is in investing)
  − Increase in accounts receivable       (working capital)
  − Increase in inventory
  + Increase in accounts payable
  + Increase in accrued liabilities
  = Cash from operating activities

Investing activities
  − Purchases of PP&E (capex)
  + Proceeds from asset sales
  = Cash from investing activities

Financing activities
  + Debt issued            − Debt repaid
  + Equity issued          − Dividends paid   − Share buybacks
  = Cash from financing activities

Net change in cash = Operating + Investing + Financing
Beginning cash + Net change in cash = Ending cash
```

## Working-capital sign rules (indirect method, operating section)
| Balance-sheet change | Effect on cash | Sign |
|---|---|---|
| Current **asset** increases (AR, inventory, prepaid) | uses cash | subtract |
| Current **asset** decreases | frees cash | add |
| Current **liability** increases (AP, accrued) | source of cash | add |
| Current **liability** decreases | uses cash | subtract |

Mnemonic: assets and cash move **opposite**; liabilities and cash move **together**.

## Cash flow statement — direct method
Lists actual operating cash flows instead of reconciling from net income:
```
Cash received from customers
− Cash paid to suppliers
− Cash paid to employees
− Interest paid
− Income taxes paid
= Cash from operating activities
```
The direct and indirect methods yield the **same** operating cash total; only the operating section's
presentation differs. Investing and financing sections are identical under both methods.

## Articulation / tie-out checklist
1. **Net income** on the income statement = the net income line that opens the CFS operating section, and rolls
   into **retained earnings**: beginning RE + net income − dividends = ending RE on the balance sheet.
2. **Net change in cash** on the CFS = ending balance-sheet cash − beginning balance-sheet cash.
3. **Ending cash** on the CFS = the **cash** line on the ending balance sheet.
4. **Balance sheet balances**: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
5. Depreciation appears three ways in agreement: an expense on the IS, an add-back on the CFS, and an increase in
   accumulated depreciation on the BS.

If any check fails, the difference localizes the error — trace it; never plug it.

## Accrual-to-cash conversion
Convert an accrual figure to cash by adjusting for the working-capital change behind it:
- **Cash collected from customers** = revenue − increase in accounts receivable (+ decrease).
- **Cash paid to suppliers** = COGS + increase in inventory − increase in accounts payable.
- **Cash paid for an expense** = expense + increase in the related prepaid − increase in the related accrual.

This is the same mechanic the indirect method applies line by line.

## GAAP vs IFRS notes (neutral)
- **Statement names:** IFRS uses *statement of financial position* (balance sheet) and *statement of profit or
  loss* (income statement); US GAAP uses balance sheet and income statement. Content aligns.
- **Cash flow classification of interest and dividends:** US GAAP generally fixes interest paid/received and
  dividends received in operating, dividends paid in financing. IFRS permits a **policy choice** — interest and
  dividends paid may be operating or financing; interest and dividends received may be operating or investing.
- **Method:** both frameworks permit the direct or indirect method and both encourage direct; indirect is more
  common in practice.
- The **articulation and tie-out logic is identical** across frameworks; only classification and presentation vary.
