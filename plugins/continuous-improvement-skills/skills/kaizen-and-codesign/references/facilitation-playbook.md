# Kaizen / co-design facilitation playbook (reference)

Practical scaffolding to charter, run, and follow up a kaizen event or co-design session for a
finance/accounting/treasury process.

## Contents
- Kaizen charter
- A typical event agenda
- Facilitation techniques for equal voice
- Rapid PDCA inside the event
- Respect-for-people in practice
- Follow-up / sustainment

## Kaizen charter
- **Process in scope:** <one narrow, real process> · **Boundaries:** starts/ends where <…>
- **Objective (measurable):** <e.g. cut this task's cycle time from 40 to 20 min>
- **Dates / time-box:** <e.g. 2 days> · **Sponsor (removes obstacles / authorizes):** <…>
- **Participants:** the **operators who do the work**, a **downstream customer** of the output, and a
  **decision-maker** who can approve changes in the room.
- **Out of scope:** <what you will deliberately not touch>

## A typical event agenda
1. Kickoff: charter, objective, ground rules (equal voice, problem = process not person).
2. **Gemba walk:** observe the real work; gather facts; talk to the doers.
3. Current-state view: quick map or the relevant slice of a value stream map.
4. Idea generation: silent write → affinity group → dot-vote to prioritize.
5. **Rapid PDCA loops:** prototype → try on real work → observe → adjust (repeat).
6. Draft the new **standard work**.
7. Close: results vs. objective, owners/dates for open items, 30/60-day follow-up scheduled.

## Facilitation techniques for equal voice
- **Round-robin** so everyone speaks, not just the confident.
- **Silent idea-writing (brainwriting)** before discussion — decouples idea quality from status/volume.
- **Affinity grouping** to cluster ideas without early judgment.
- **Dot-voting / multivoting** to prioritize the vital few.
- Facilitator owns the *process*; the group owns the *content*. Protect the quietest operator's idea.

## Rapid PDCA inside the event
Bias to *trying*, not deliberating. Plan a small change → Do it on real work that day → Check what
happened → Act (keep/adjust) and loop again. Several fast, reversible loops beat one big untested rollout.

## Respect-for-people in practice
- Frame every problem as a **process** problem, never a person's fault — blame makes people hide problems.
- Credit the people whose ideas were used; make the improvement theirs.
- This is what makes the team bring the next idea — kaizen is a habit the event seeds, not a one-off.

## Follow-up / sustainment
- Capture the improved method as standard work (`continuous-improvement-skills:standard-work`).
- Assign owners and dates to every open action; set the metric to watch.
- Schedule a **30/60-day check**: did the change hold, and did it hit the objective? If not, cycle again.
