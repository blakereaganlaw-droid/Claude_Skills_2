# Kaizen and co-design

## When to use
- Planning or facilitating a **kaizen event** (a focused, time-boxed improvement workshop) on a real
  process.
- Running a **co-design / participatory** session where the workers and customers design the change
  together, not consultants for them.
- Facilitating any continuous-improvement session at the gemba with rapid PDCA.
- Not for: mapping the whole value stream that scopes the event → see
  `continuous-improvement-skills:value-stream-mapping`. To capture and lock in the result afterward →
  see `continuous-improvement-skills:standard-work`.

## Do it
1. **Scope and charter the kaizen tightly.** One narrow, real process; a specific, measurable objective;
   fixed dates and boundaries; a sponsor who removes obstacles. Kaizen means *small, focused, fast* —
   a bounded problem you can move within days, not a boil-the-ocean program. See
   `references/facilitation-playbook.md`.
2. **Assemble the right room: the people who do the work + the downstream customer.** Include the actual
   operators (not just supervisors), a downstream "customer" who receives the output, and someone who can
   authorize changes on the spot. This mix is the whole design — the workers hold the knowledge, the
   customer holds the need.
3. **Go to the gemba first.** Start where the work actually happens: observe the real process, talk to
   the people doing it, gather facts. Design from what *is*, not from a conference-room guess. Grounding
   the group in shared, observed reality prevents solving an imagined problem.
4. **Facilitate for equal voice.** Use techniques that surface every participant's knowledge — round-
   robins, silent idea-writing before discussion, affinity grouping, dot-voting to prioritize. Protect
   the quietest operator's idea from the loudest manager's. The facilitator owns the *process*; the group
   owns the *content*.
5. **Run rapid PDCA inside the event.** Don't just plan — **try**. Prototype a change, test it on real
   work that same day, observe, adjust, and try again. Several fast small loops beat one big rollout;
   the event's power is testing ideas live where the work is.
6. **Capture standard work and a follow-up plan before you close.** Document the improved method as
   standard work (see `continuous-improvement-skills:standard-work`), assign owners and dates for
   anything unfinished, set the metric to watch, and schedule a 30/60-day check. An event with no
   standard and no follow-up reverts.
7. **Honor respect-for-people throughout.** Frame every problem as a process problem, never a person
   problem; give credit to the people whose ideas were used; and make the improvement *theirs*. This is
   the principle that makes people bring the next idea, too.

## Why / learn
Those closest to the work design the best, most durable improvements — that is the load-bearing belief
behind both kaizen and co-design, and everything in the method follows from it. The operator who does a
task 200 times a day knows its real failure modes, workarounds, and constraints in a way no outside
analyst can reconstruct from a diagram, so a solution designed *with* them starts from truer facts and
skips the failures a remote design would blunder into. But the deeper reason is ownership: **people
support what they help create.** A change handed down is complied with grudgingly and quietly reverts the
moment attention moves on; a change the team designed is *theirs* to defend, so it sticks. That is why you
go to the gemba (design from reality, not imagination), why you put the downstream customer in the room
(so the improvement serves the actual need, not a proxy for it), and why facilitation must protect equal
voice — the best idea is often the quietest person's, and a session dominated by the highest-paid opinion
wastes the very knowledge you convened to capture. Rapid PDCA inside the event embodies the Lean bias for
*trying* over *deliberating*: a small change tested on real work today teaches more than a week of
meeting-room debate, and it's reversible. And **respect-for-people** isn't soft framing — it's the engine
of continuous improvement: treat every problem as a process problem rather than a blame hunt, credit the
people whose ideas you use, and they'll surface the next hundred improvements. Kaizen is less an event
than a habit the event is meant to seed.

## Common mistakes
- Scoping too big → nothing moves in the time-box. Kaizen is small, focused, and fast.
- Filling the room with managers, not the actual operators → you lose the real knowledge. Invite the doers.
- Leaving the downstream customer out → you improve for a proxy need. Put the receiver in the room.
- Planning without trying → analysis paralysis. Run rapid PDCA; test on real work the same day.
- The loudest voice dominates → the best (quiet) idea is lost. Facilitate for equal voice.
- Closing with no standard work or follow-up → it reverts in weeks. Capture the standard; schedule a check.
- Framing problems as people's faults → people hide problems and stop contributing. Respect-for-people first.
