## How to read a permitted-investments statute

Work through the statute with these questions; the answers become your permitted-list table.

1. **Scope** — which entities and which funds does this section govern? (States often have
   separate sections for the state treasurer, local governments, universities, and special
   authorities.)
2. **Instrument list** — what is expressly permitted? Note qualifiers on each: issuer type,
   rating floor, maximum maturity, domestic/branch requirements for deposits.
3. **Limits** — per-issuer, per-instrument-class, or portfolio-percentage caps; any
   weighted-average-maturity cap.
4. **Deposit conditions** — what makes a bank deposit legal (insured, collateralized, or in
   a qualified public depository under the state's program)?
5. **Delegation and policy requirements** — does the statute require a written, adopted
   investment policy? Who may execute?
6. **Pooling authority** — is joining the state LGIP/treasurer's pool authorized for your
   entity type?
7. **Cross-references** — definitions sections, the public depository act, and any
   university- or system-specific enabling statute that overrides the general one.

Record for each pot of money: the statute section(s), the effective date checked, and who
confirmed it (analyst + counsel/policy office where material).

## Collateralization mechanics: pledge vs pool

**Direct pledge model**
- The depository pledges specific securities against your deposits, at or above the
  statutory margin (states commonly require collateral value to exceed the uninsured
  balance by a set percentage — use your state's number, not a remembered one).
- Securities sit with an **independent third-party custodian** (often a Federal Reserve or
  correspondent safekeeping arrangement) under a written collateral/security agreement
  naming your entity. A properly executed agreement matters: in a bank failure, the
  receiver's rules on enforceability decide whether you are secured or unsecured.
- You (or your agent) receive safekeeping statements and revalue the collateral on a set
  cycle; you can demand substitution or additional collateral when values fall or balances
  spike.

**State collateral pool model**
- Participating banks pledge into a single state-administered pool covering all public
  depositors in the program, with the administrator setting pledge levels (often tiered to
  the bank's condition) and handling monitoring centrally.
- Your work shifts from monitoring securities to verifying: the bank's current membership
  and good standing, what the pool covers, any loss-allocation provisions, and the reporting
  you must still file (many programs require depositors to report balances).

**Choosing/verifying**: pool membership simplifies operations; direct pledge gives direct
control. Either way, the deposit is legal only if the arrangement matches the statute — an
uncollateralized balance over insurance is a finding, not a detail.

securities; safekeeping statements that name the bank instead of you.

## LGIP / state pool due-diligence checklist

Before joining, and on a recurring cycle after:
- Governing authority and your entity's eligibility to participate.
- The pool's investment policy: permitted instruments, WAM/WAL limits, credit standards.
- Valuation basis: amortized-cost/stable-value (and the criteria the pool must meet to use
  it — GASB has specific requirements for external investment pools) vs floating NAV.
- Portfolio transparency: holdings reports, frequency, and whether you actually read them.
- Rating (if rated), administrator/manager, custodian, and audit reports.
- Liquidity terms: same-day/next-day limits, notice requirements, gates in stress.
- Operational: transaction cutoffs, interest distribution, reporting for your GL posting.
- Concentration: what share of your liquidity depends on this single vehicle.

## Bond proceeds: the extra layer

Unspent bond proceeds are public funds *plus*: the trust indenture may narrow permitted
investments further, and federal tax law (arbitrage yield restriction and rebate) governs
the economics of investing tax-exempt proceeds. Track proceeds separately, follow the
indenture's list, and involve bond counsel/financial advisor before any strategy decision.

## Worked example framing: Tennessee

For a Tennessee public university, the working stack typically looks like: Tennessee's
public-funds investment statutes and the state's public depository / collateral-pool
framework at the base; system (e.g. UT system) and institution investment policies in the
middle; then operational choices — the state-run LGIP as a liquidity tier, collateralized
deposits at qualified depositories, and any separately governed pools (endowment, bond
proceeds). Treat this as the *shape* of the answer: confirm each layer's current text —
statute sections, collateral pool program rules, LGIP terms, and system policy — with the
policy office or counsel before relying on it.
