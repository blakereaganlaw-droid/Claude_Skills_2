# Federal cost allowability

## When to use
- Screening costs or billed amounts on federal awards: "can this be charged/billed?",
  invoice-line reviews, adjustment and write-off proposals, questioned-cost triage.
- Explaining why a specific cost type is (un)allowable, with the citation.
- Not for: the overall UG frame and thresholds → see
  `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:uniform-guidance-federal-core`. For personnel/effort support →
  see `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:federal-effort-reporting-basics`. For portfolio-level risk
  scanning → see `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:federal-sponsored-ar-compliance-risk`.

## Do it
1. **Run every cost through the §200.403 factor tests — all must pass:**
   - **Necessary and reasonable** for performance of the federal award, and **allocable** to it.
   - **Conforms to limitations/exclusions** in the cost principles *and the award terms* (the
     NOA can be stricter than the CFR).
   - **Consistent with institutional policies** that apply uniformly to federal and
     non-federal activities alike.
   - **Consistent treatment** — a cost type treated as indirect elsewhere can't be charged
     direct here (and vice versa).
   - **Determined in accordance with GAAP** (limited exceptions for states/local
     governments/tribes).
   - **Not double-charged or used as cost sharing** on any other federally financed program.
   - **Adequately documented** — the test that fails most often in practice.
2. **Apply the reasonableness standard (§200.404):** would a **prudent person** under the
   circumstances at the time have incurred this cost at this price? Market rates, institutional
   norms, arm's-length dealing, and no significant deviation from established practice are the
   working indicators. Reasonableness is judged at the time of the decision, not in hindsight.
3. **Test allocability (§200.405):** the cost is chargeable **in accordance with relative
   benefits received** — incurred specifically for the award, or benefiting both the award and
   other work in **proportions that can be approximated** (allocate proportionally), or
   necessary to overall operations (then it's indirect). A cost can be perfectly legitimate
   and still unallocable *to this award* — allocability failures are transfer candidates, not
   necessarily unallowable costs.
4. **Check the special rules before concluding:** **administrative/clerical salaries are
   normally indirect** — direct-charging requires *all* §200.413(c) conditions (integral to
   the project, individuals identified, budgeted or prior-approved, not also recovered as
   indirect); equipment purchases often need prior approval; travel follows institutional
   policy plus award terms; participant support costs need agency approval and are excluded
   from MTDC. `references/allowability-worksheet.md` carries the special-rules table and the
   selected-items quick list (§200.420–.476).
5. **Screen against the always-unallowable list**, with the AR-critical one first:
   **bad debts (§200.426) — including related collection and legal costs** — can never be
   charged to a federal award; alcohol, entertainment, lobbying, fines/penalties follow.
   For write-off proposals this is decisive: uncollectible federal AR moves to non-sponsored
   funds (see `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:federal-sponsored-ar-compliance-risk` for the
   process side).
6. **Translate failures into AR consequences.** If unallowable costs were already **billed**:
   expect credit memos, refunds to the agency (§200.410, often with interest), or movement of
   the cost to non-sponsored funds — and prior revenue/AR recognition unwinds accordingly.
   Flag every failed item as a potential **questioned cost** with the exposure math (direct +
   fringe + F&A; the effort skill's multiplier applies).
7. **Deliver the checklist verdict:** per item — pass/fail per test, the CFR citation, and the
   recommended action ("review supporting documentation," "obtain/verify prior approval,"
   "transfer to appropriate fund," "prepare for potential questioned cost") — with the
   standing disclaimer that the regulation, award terms, and institutional/agency policy
   govern, and that this is analytical screening, not a compliance determination.

## Why / learn
The cost principles are a *filter stack*, and the order matters: allowability (§200.403) asks
"may this type of cost ever be charged," reasonableness (§200.404) asks "was this instance
prudent," and allocability (§200.405) asks "does *this award* owe it" — a cost must survive
all three, and each failure has a different remedy (never charge / reduce or justify /
transfer elsewhere). Keeping the three apart is what makes review actionable: most flagged
items aren't scandals, they're allocability or documentation problems with clean fixes. The
consistent-treatment and uniform-policy tests exist because the government pays thousands of
institutions under one rulebook and refuses to subsidize creative cost accounting — you can't
call a cost indirect for the rate proposal and direct for the invoice. The
administrative-salary rule is the same logic concentrated: those salaries are already in the
F&A rate, so direct-charging them without the §200.413 conditions is double recovery. And the
bad-debt rule closes the system for AR purposes: the government pre-refuses to fund collection
failure, so allowability review is where a write-off proposal on a federal award gets stopped
*before* it becomes a §200.426 violation — which is why this skill sits in the billing path,
not just the compliance office.

## Common mistakes
- Running one blended "is it allowable" test → keep §200.403/404/405 separate; each failure has a different fix.
- Judging reasonableness in hindsight → the prudent-person test applies at the time of the decision.
- Treating allocability failures as unallowable costs → often legitimate costs charged to the wrong place; transfer, don't condemn.
- Direct-charging admin/clerical salaries without all §200.413 conditions → double recovery against the F&A rate; check all four.
- Forgetting the award terms layer → the NOA and agency policy can prohibit what the CFR permits; read both.
- Passing costs with thin documentation → "adequately documented" is a §200.403 factor, and the most-failed one; no support, no pass.
- Charging collection/legal costs of chasing federal AR to the award → unallowable alongside the bad debt itself (§200.426).
- Presenting the screen as a determination → it's analysis; compliance office, agency, and award terms decide.
