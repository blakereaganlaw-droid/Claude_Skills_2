# Federal effort reporting basics

## When to use
- Explaining how personnel costs may be charged to federal awards and what records must stand
  behind them.
- Assessing AR/billing risk on federal awards where salaries dominate the charges (they
  usually do), or interpreting effort-related findings.
- Not for: the broader allowability framework and always-unallowable costs → see
  `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:uniform-guidance-federal-core`. For how questioned costs
  affect draws/refunds → see `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:federal-billing-cash-management`.

## Do it
1. **State the §200.430 standard in its four working parts.** Compensation charged to federal
   awards must be: **(a) reasonable** for the services rendered; **(b) consistent** with the
   institution's established policy *applied uniformly to federal and non-federal activities
   alike*; **(c) supported by records that accurately reflect the work performed**; and
   **(d)** for institutions of higher education, conformant with the IHE-specific rules
   (IBS — institutional base salary — as the charging basis, among others).
2. **Know what the records must do (not what form they take).** Uniform Guidance is
   principles-based: records (whatever the system — effort certification, plan-confirmation,
   payroll distribution) must be supported by **internal controls**, be **incorporated into
   official records**, **reasonably reflect total activity** for which the employee is
   compensated — **not exceeding 100%** across all activities — and **support the
   distribution** of salary among specific activities/awards. The 100% rule is the famous
   one: effort is a share of *total* institutional activity, not of a 40-hour week.
3. **Use budget estimates only as interim scaffolding.** Charging salaries per the proposal
   budget is permitted for interim accounting **only if** the system produces reasonable
   approximations and **significant changes are adjusted to actual activity** — a budget
   number never certified against reality is exactly what audits question. The
   estimate-then-true-up cycle must demonstrably happen.
4. **Connect effort to the AR you're analyzing.** Personnel costs typically dominate federal
   research awards (often 60%+ of direct costs), so the collectibility of federal AR — and the
   durability of *already-collected* amounts — rests on effort support: **questioned personnel
   costs unwind previously billed/drawn amounts** (refunds under §200.410, debts under
   §200.346). In AR terms: high personnel density + weak effort support = billed AR that
   isn't final. Flag awards where salary share is high and certification status is unknown.
5. **Screen for the classic red flags** (each maps to a §200.430 element):
   effort charged over 100% across awards; salary charged 100% to one award for someone with
   teaching/admin duties; distributions that never change (estimates never trued up);
   certifications signed by someone without suitable means of verifying the work; charges above
   an agency salary cap (e.g. NIH) not cost-shared. `references/effort-essentials.md` carries
   the checklist and the questioned-cost math.
6. **Deliver concisely:** the standard explained, the red-flag scan of the data at hand, the
   AR-impact statement (what's at risk if support fails), and a pointer to the allowability
   layer — item screening via `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:federal-cost-allowability`, the
   broader frame via `sponsored-projects-ar-skills:uniform-guidance-federal-core` — with the
   standing disclaimer that the regulation, agency terms, and institutional policy govern.

## Why / learn
Effort is where federal cost compliance is won or lost for one structural reason: salaries are
the biggest cost pool *and* the only major one whose measurement is inherently judgmental — a
lab supply either was or wasn't bought, but "40% of Dr. Chen's total activity" is an estimate
of an estimate. §200.430 responds not by demanding fake precision (it explicitly says the
records need not be exact) but by demanding a *system*: uniform policy, internal controls,
official records, a total-activity denominator, and true-ups from estimate to actual. The 100%
rule embodies the key insight — effort is a *share*, not hours, so the denominator is
everything the institution pays the person to do, and overcommitment across awards is
arithmetic fraud even when every individual charge looks plausible. For the receivables
analyst, the punchline is that federal AR quality is downstream of payroll hygiene: a dollar
billed for unsupported effort is a dollar the institution may repay with interest years later,
which is why "billed and collected" is a weaker state on federal awards than anywhere else in
AR — and why an effort red flag in project data is an AR finding, not just an HR one.

## Common mistakes
- Reading effort as hours out of 40 → it's a percentage of *total* institutional activity; the denominator is everything.
- Letting budget-based charges ride without true-up → interim estimates must adjust to actuals; a never-changing distribution is the red flag.
- Treating certification as a signature ritual → the certifier needs suitable means of verifying the work happened as charged.
- Different rigor for federal vs non-federal salary charging → §200.430 requires uniform institutional policy.
- Ignoring agency salary caps → over-cap salary is charged to the award only up to the cap; the rest is institutional (cost-shared) expense.
- Assuming collected federal AR is final → questioned effort claws back prior billings (§200.410/§200.346); durability depends on support.
- Scanning AR risk without checking personnel density → salary-heavy awards carry the compliance tail; weight the scan accordingly.
